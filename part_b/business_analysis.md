PART B1
Ans a). The business wants to know — for each store, every month — which promotion will result in the highest number of items being sold. To answer this, we can build a machine learning model that looks at historical data and learns which combinations of store type, location, time of year, and promotion tend to produce the best sales.
The target variable — the thing we want the model to predict — is items sold. Since this is a number that can take many values (not just yes or no), this is a regression problem. We are predicting a quantity, not a category.
The features — the inputs we give the model — would include the type of promotion being run, the store's location (urban, semi-urban, or rural), the store's size, how many competitors are nearby, the month of the year, whether there are festival days or weekends in that period, and how the store has been performing in recent months.
Once the model is trained, the team can test all five promotions for each store before the month begins, see which one the model predicts will sell the most, and go with that. The model essentially becomes a decision-support tool for the marketing team.

Ans b).At first glance, revenue seems like the obvious thing to maximise — after all, the business needs to make money. 
But , items sold is more honest parameter that directly tells us how well the promotion drove customer purchases, regardless of price. 
For example, imagine two promotions. One is a flat discount on expensive items — it brings in high revenue but only a few customers buy. The other is a free gift offer that gets a lot more people to purchase, but each item is cheaper. Revenue-wise, they might look similar. But the free gift promotion clearly did a better job of attracting customers and moving stock. So ,if we train the model on revenue , it would lead to wrong decisions.
The general lesson here is that the target variable should measure exactly what you are trying to influence like revenue conflates promotion impact with pricing strategy while Items sold isolates the behaviour the business actually wants to influence.

Ans c).A single global model trained across all 50 stores is too blunt. If we force one model to learn from all stores together, it ends up learning a middle ground that does not accurately represent any specific store. It is like trying to write one menu for a fine dining restaurant and a roadside dhaba at the same time — the result pleases neither. 
A better approach is to train separate models for each location type — one for urban stores, one for semi-urban, and one for rural. Each model learns from stores that share similar characteristics, so its predictions are far more relevant and accurate for those stores. This way, the model for rural stores understands what works in that context, and the model for urban stores understands its own dynamics — without one interfering with the other. If store-level data is sufficient, this can go further with store-cluster models — grouping stores by footfall, size, and competition density using clustering (like K-Means).

PART B2
Ans a). We have four separate tables — transactions, store attributes, promotion details, and a calendar. The goal is to combine them into one clean dataset where each row represents one store, one month, and one promotion along with all the relevant information.
The first step is to summarise the transactions table. Instead of keeping every individual purchase as a separate row, we group by store and month and calculate the total items sold, the number of purchases made, and the average number of items per purchase. This gives us one row per store per month.
Next, we bring in the store attributes by matching on the store ID. This adds information like store size, location type, footfall, and competition density to each row.
Then we add the promotion details — whichever promotion was running in that store during that month — by matching on the promotion identifier.
Finally, we bring in the calendar by matching on the month and year. From the calendar, instead of keeping individual day flags, we calculate how many weekend days and how many festival days occurred in that month and add those as numeric columns.

Ans b). Before jumping into modelling, it is important to spend time understanding the data. Here are four key analyses that would guide the decisions made during modelling.
The first is to look at how items sold varies across the five promotion types. Plotting the distribution of sales for each promotion shows us whether some promotions are consistently stronger than others, or whether the results are mixed and context-dependent. This immediately tells us whether a simple "one best promotion for everyone" exists, or whether the answer depends on other factors.
The second is to compare promotion performance across location types. For example, does BOGO work better in urban stores than rural ones? If the same promotion gives very different results in different locations, that interaction needs to be built into the model. This finding would directly influence whether we build separate models per location or add a combined promotion-location feature.
The third is to look at trends over time. Plotting total items sold month by month reveals seasonal patterns — peaks around festivals, slower months in off-season periods. This confirms that the month of the year and festival indicators are important features, and might also suggest that including last month's sales as an input would help the model understand momentum.
The fourth is to check relationships between items sold and numeric factors like competition density and footfall. If stores with high footfall consistently sell more, footfall is a strong feature. If there is no real pattern, it might not be worth including. This analysis also checks whether two input features are too similar to each other, which can sometimes confuse a model and reduce its accuracy.

Ans c).The result is a model that **underestimates the impact of promotions** and produces unreliable predictions precisely where the business needs confidence most. A more advanced option is to build two separate models — one trained only on no-promotion weeks to understand baseline sales, and another trained only on promotion weeks. The difference between their predictions gives the estimated extra sales generated by the promotion. This "lift" figure is often more useful for the marketing team than a raw prediction, because it tells them how much the promotion actually adds on top of what would have sold anyway.

PART B3
Ans a). With three years of monthly data across 50 stores, the right way to split the data is by time — not randomly. The most recent months become the test set, and everything before that becomes the training set. If we pick records randomly for training and testing, the model might train on data from December 2023 and test on data from March 2022. That means it is essentially being tested on the past after seeing the future, which gives a falsely optimistic picture of how it will actually perform when deployed. 
For metrics, RMSE — Root Mean Squared Error tells us, on average, how far off the model's predictions are from the actual items sold.
Then comes MAE — Mean Absolute Error that gives a simpler, more intuitive number — on average, how many items does the model's prediction differ from reality? If MAE is 80, it means the model's recommendation is typically off by about 80 items. 

Ans b). This is actually a strength of the model, not a flaw — and explaining it clearly to the marketing team is important so they trust and use the recommendations confidently.
Feature importance tells us which inputs had the biggest influence on the model's output. For Store 12, we can look at which features drove the December recommendation towards Loyalty Points Bonus and which drove the March recommendation towards Flat Discount.
In December, the likely drivers are festival days, the fact that it is a peak shopping month, and perhaps Store 12's historical pattern of customers making multiple purchases during that period. Loyalty Points Bonus tends to reward repeat purchases — customers accumulate points across several trips, which fits naturally with the higher footfall and shopping frequency that December brings. The model learned this pattern from past data and is applying it here.
In March, those festival and seasonal boosts are absent. It is a quieter month where customers are more deal-driven and looking for immediate value. A Flat Discount offers instant gratification — customers see the price drop and buy. The model learned that in these lower-footfall, lower-energy months, immediate discounts outperform loyalty-based offers for this particular store.
To communicate this to the marketing team, the simplest approach is to show them a short breakdown for each recommendation — something like: "The top three factors driving this recommendation for Store 12 in December are the number of festival days this month, the store's high footfall in winter, and the strong historical performance of loyalty-based promotions during this period." This makes the recommendation feel grounded and explainable rather than a black-box output, which builds confidence in acting on it.

Ans c). After training, the model is saved as a file on a server. At the start of each month, someone prepares the input data — basically filling in the details for each store such as store size, location, expected festival days, weekends, and which promotions are available. This data is fed into the saved model, and the model spits out a predicted sales number for every store-promotion combination. Whichever promotion gets the highest predicted sales for a store becomes the recommendation for that month. These recommendations are then shared with the marketing team through a simple report or dashboard.
Monitoring is what keeps the system healthy over time. The most important thing to track is prediction accuracy — each month, once actual sales come in, we compare them against what the model predicted. If the gap starts widening consistently over several months, it is a signal that the model is no longer capturing current reality well. This can happen because customer behaviour shifts, a new competitor enters the market, or the business introduces a new promotion type the model has never seen.
